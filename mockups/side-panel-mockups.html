What research exists on the effectiveness of AI text detection tools in 2026? I need specific numbers I can cite.

Claude

Here's what the current research shows:

Accuracy on raw AI text: Weber-Wulff et al. (2023) tested 14 detectors and found accuracy rates between 55–96% on unmodified AI text. GPTZero was among the best at ~99% for raw GPT-4 output.

After paraphrasing/humanizing: Sadasivan et al. (2023) showed that simple paraphrasing attacks reduce all detector accuracy to near-random (50–60%). The 18% figure you're using likely comes from Liang et al. (2023) testing against DIPPER paraphrasing.

2025–2026 developments: The MLA-CCCC Working Paper (Jan 2025) formally recommended against using detection tools for academic integrity decisions, calling them "not appropriate as the sole basis for any consequential decision."

You

Good. What's the strongest counterargument to my position that detection is a dead end?

Claude

The strongest counter is the "good enough for flagging" argument: detection doesn't need to be perfect, it just needs to be useful as a screening tool.

The case goes like this: even at 70–80% accuracy, detection creates a cost for deception. It's the difference between a lock that can be picked and no lock at all. Most people won't bother picking the lock.

To address this in your article, you need the asymmetric harm argument: when detection gets it wrong, the false positive (accusing a human writer) is far more damaging than the false negative (missing AI text). The Liang et al. finding about bias against non-native speakers makes this concrete — it's not hypothetical harm, it's documented disparate impact.

You should also note that the "flagging" argument assumes someone reviews the flags. In practice, automated systems act on detection scores directly — Turnitin scores trigger academic misconduct proceedings without human review in many institutions.

  • Auto-generated thread titles from the first user message (editable on click)
  • "+ New" starts a fresh conversation with document context
  • Threads are per-document — switching documents shows that document's threads
  • All threads are persisted in the database and logged in the audit trail
  • Thread switching is instant — messages loaded from local cache, synced with DB
  • The audit trail captures which thread each interaction belongs to
