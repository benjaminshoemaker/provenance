In a world where anyone can generate a passable blog post in seconds, I find myself returning to the keyboard with renewed purpose. Not because I think AI writing is bad — it isn't — but because the act of writing has always been, for me, an act of thinking.

Writing forces you to confront the gaps in your understanding. When you sit down to explain something, you quickly discover what you don't actually know. This is the fundamental tension that AI writing tools create: they give you the output without requiring the process.

I've been experimenting with AI assistance for the past six months. According to a 2025 survey by the Content Marketing Institute, 73% of content creators now use AI tools in some capacity, up from 48% just one year prior. The numbers tell one story, but my experience tells another.

Here's what I've learned: the most effective use of AI in writing isn't generation — it's refinement. Using AI to tighten a sentence, suggest a stronger verb, or identify logical gaps preserves your voice while leveraging the tool's strengths.

What this badge certifies

Certifies: The writing process for this piece was conducted in Provenance, and the complete record of that process — including all AI interactions — is available for review.

Does not certify: That no AI was used outside the tool, that the ideas are original, that the published version matches this snapshot, or that no other tools were used alongside Provenance.

How the AI percentage is calculated

The AI-generated percentage represents the proportion of final document characters that originated from accepted AI suggestions and remained substantially unchanged (<20% modification). Characters typed directly, or AI text modified by 20% or more, are classified as human-written. This metric can be gamed and represents what the tool observed, not a guarantee of authorship.
